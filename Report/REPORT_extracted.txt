RESEARCH AND OPTIMIZATION OF VIETNAMESE PRINTED AND HANDWRITTEN TEXT RECOGNITION SYSTEM USING TROCR ARCHITECTURE
ABSTRACT
Text recognition remains a fundamental challenge in document digitization, particularly for Vietnamese, where rich diacritical marks, tonal variations, and significant differences between handwritten and printed forms impose unique demands on recognition systems. Existing approaches, typically based on CNN-RNN architectures with CTC decoding, struggle to capture long-range contextual dependencies and often require fragmented, language-specific post-processing.
This work presents a unified Vietnamese text recognition framework built upon TrOCR, a Transformer-based encoder-decoder architecture combining a Vision Transformer (ViT) encoder with a RoBERTa-based autoregressive decoder. To adapt the model from its original English-centric, square-image pre-training to the wide rectangular geometry of Vietnamese text lines, we apply bicubic interpolation to reshape the ViT positional embeddings from a 24×24 grid to an 8×96 configuration, enabling a 128×1536 input resolution without discarding pre-trained spatial priors. The decoder vocabulary is extended with 254 Vietnamese-specific tokens to eliminate suboptimal byte-level fragmentation of diacritical characters.
A multi-stage curriculum training strategy is proposed to progressively increase sequence complexity: starting from isolated words, advancing through dynamically constructed pseudo-lines, and culminating in mixed-domain line-level fine-tuning with a printed data replay buffer (70% handwritten, 30% printed per batch). Elastic Weight Consolidation (EWC) with a Fisher Information Matrix computed over printed data is jointly applied during this mixed replay stage to prevent catastrophic forgetting of printed text accuracy while adapting to handwritten samples. Additional techniques including Layer-wise Learning Rate Decay (LLRD), mixed-precision training, and gradient accumulation are integrated to ensure stable convergence within a single-GPU environment.
The recognition model is embedded within a complete end-to-end OCR pipeline comprising image preprocessing (CLAHE enhancement and Hough-based deskewing), text detection (DBNet), line segmentation (DBSCAN clustering), TrOCR inference, and a multi-stage post-processing module featuring deterministic hallucination sanitization and geographic entity correction using a 10,500-entry Vietnamese administrative gazetteer.
Experimental evaluation on a combined test set of 4,477 samples across multiple Vietnamese datasets yields an overall Character Error Rate (CER) of 2.86% and Word Error Rate (WER) of 7.92%, with printed text achieving 1.17% CER and handwritten text achieving 10.57% CER. These results demonstrate that the proposed framework provides an effective and scalable solution for Vietnamese text recognition in real-world document digitization tasks.

INTRODUCTION
Background and Motivation
In the era of digital transformation, digitizing archived documents and personal records plays a pivotal role in building intelligent management systems. However, Vietnamese text recognition, particularly for both handwritten and printed text, remains a significant challenge due to the linguistic complexity of tone marks, high character stroke connectivity, and the diversity in writing styles.
Most existing approaches rely on traditional architectures such as CRNN combined with CTC, which often struggle to capture long-range dependencies and complex visual patterns. In addition, available Vietnamese datasets are typically fragmented and lack standardization, limiting the generalization ability of current models. Despite recent advances in Transformer-based methods, their effectiveness for Vietnamese text recognition, especially in a unified setting for both handwritten and printed text, has not been fully explored.
Motivated by these challenges, this study investigates the effectiveness of the Transformer-based architecture TrOCR for Vietnamese text recognition, aiming to evaluate its capability in modeling contextual information and improving recognition performance across diverse text types.

Research Problem
Despite recent advances in optical character recognition, Vietnamese text recognition remains a challenging problem. The difficulty arises from several factors, including the complex system of diacritics, high variability in handwriting styles, and the presence of both handwritten and printed text in real-world documents.
Existing approaches, primarily based on CNN-RNN architectures such as CRNN combined with CTC, have shown effectiveness in structured and clean text scenarios. However, these methods often struggle to capture long-range contextual dependencies and are less robust when dealing with irregular layouts or highly variable handwriting. In addition, the lack of large-scale, standardized Vietnamese datasets further limits the performance and generalization capability of current models.
While Transformer-based models have demonstrated strong performance in various vision and language tasks, their application to Vietnamese text recognition, particularly in a unified framework that handles both handwritten and printed text, remains underexplored.

Scope of Study
This study focuses on line-level Vietnamese text recognition, covering both handwritten and printed text. More complex scenarios, such as document layout analysis, mathematical expressions, or technical drawings, are beyond the scope of this work.
The research is limited to the Vietnamese language, including its full system of diacritics and special characters, without extending to multilingual settings.
In terms of evaluation, the study considers both recognition accuracy and computational efficiency under practical hardware constraints, aiming to ensure applicability in real-world scenarios with limited computational resources.

RELATED WORKS
CNN-RNN Based Methods
Traditional optical character recognition (OCR) systems are predominantly based on convolutional and recurrent neural network architectures. A representative approach is the Convolutional Recurrent Neural Network (CRNN) combined with Connectionist Temporal Classification (CTC), where convolutional layers are used for feature extraction and recurrent layers model the sequential nature of text. CTC enables alignment-free sequence prediction, making it suitable for variable-length text recognition tasks.
These methods have demonstrated strong performance in structured and relatively clean text scenarios due to their computational efficiency and simplicity. However, they are inherently limited in capturing long-range dependencies and global contextual information. As a result, their performance degrades when applied to complex handwritten text, irregular layouts, or languages with rich diacritics such as Vietnamese.
OCR Frameworks and Practical Systems
In practical applications, end-to-end OCR frameworks such as PaddleOCR and VietOCR have been widely adopted. PaddleOCR integrates multiple components, including text detection, orientation classification, and text recognition, and supports various backbone architectures such as DBNet for detection and SVTR for recognition. Its modular design and efficiency make it suitable for real-world deployment scenarios.
Similarly, VietOCR is designed specifically for Vietnamese text recognition, incorporating convolutional feature extraction and Transformer-based sequence modeling. It demonstrates strong performance on Vietnamese datasets, particularly for printed text and moderately complex handwriting.
Despite their effectiveness, these frameworks typically rely on multi-stage pipelines and are primarily optimized for printed or semi-structured text. Their ability to capture deep contextual relationships remains limited, and performance often deteriorates in challenging handwritten scenarios.
Transformer-based Approaches
Recent advances in deep learning have introduced Transformer-based architectures for OCR tasks. Models such as TrOCR adopt an encoder–decoder framework, where a Vision Transformer (ViT) encodes the input image into a sequence of visual tokens, and a Transformer-based decoder generates the corresponding text sequence.
By leveraging self-attention mechanisms, Transformer-based models are capable of capturing global contextual dependencies across the entire input, enabling more robust recognition of complex text patterns. Furthermore, these models can be pre-trained on large-scale synthetic datasets and fine-tuned on domain-specific data, significantly improving performance.
However, Transformer-based approaches typically require higher computational resources and are less efficient compared to traditional CNN-based methods. Additionally, their application to low-resource languages and domain-specific scenarios, such as Vietnamese handwritten text, has not been extensively explored.
Vietnamese Text Recognition Challenges
Vietnamese text recognition presents unique challenges due to linguistic and data-related factors. The Vietnamese writing system includes a rich set of diacritics, where a single character may contain multiple accent marks, increasing the complexity of character representation. In handwritten text, variations in writing styles, stroke connectivity, and spacing further complicate recognition tasks.
Moreover, existing Vietnamese OCR datasets are often fragmented, collected from different sources, and lack standardization in annotation formats. This fragmentation limits the ability of models to generalize effectively across diverse real-world scenarios. As a result, building a robust and unified OCR system for Vietnamese text remains an open challenge.
Language Models for Post-processing
To improve recognition accuracy, especially in languages with complex morphology, language models are often employed as a post-processing step. PhoBERT, a pre-trained Transformer-based language model for Vietnamese, has demonstrated strong performance in capturing contextual and semantic relationships within text.
By leveraging contextual embeddings, language models can correct errors generated by OCR systems, particularly those related to diacritics and ambiguous characters. However, this introduces additional computational overhead and increases system complexity, requiring careful integration with the OCR pipeline.
Research Gap
Despite significant progress in OCR technologies, several limitations remain in the context of Vietnamese text recognition. Traditional CNN-RNN architectures struggle to capture long-range contextual dependencies, while existing OCR frameworks are primarily optimized for printed text and rely on complex multi-stage pipelines. Although Transformer-based models such as TrOCR have shown promising results, their effectiveness in handling Vietnamese text, particularly in a unified framework that supports both handwritten and printed data, has not been fully explored.
In addition, the lack of standardized Vietnamese datasets further restricts model generalization and fair comparison across different approaches. These challenges highlight the need for a unified, context-aware OCR framework that leverages Transformer architectures while addressing the limitations of existing methods.

PROPOSED METHODOLOGY
Overview of the Proposed Pipeline
This study proposes an end-to-end pipeline for Vietnamese text recognition, designed to handle both handwritten and printed text in real-world scenarios. The pipeline consists of several stages, including image preprocessing, text detection, line segmentation, text recognition, and language-based post-processing. Each component is designed to address specific challenges in Vietnamese OCR, particularly the presence of diacritics, handwriting variability, and irregular text layouts.

Figure 3.1 Overview of the propose Vietnamese text recognition pipeline
Step 1: Image Preprocessing
In the first stage, input images are preprocessed to enhance visual quality and improve recognition performance. Specifically, Contrast Limited Adaptive Histogram Equalization (CLAHE) is applied to enhance local contrast while preserving fine details. In addition, a deskewing technique based on the Hough Line Transform is used to correct image orientation.
These preprocessing steps help mitigate common issues such as uneven illumination, shadows, and tilted text, which are frequently observed in handwritten documents and real-world image captures.
Step 2: Text Detection
For text detection, this study employs the DBNet architecture (db_resnet50) via the docTR library for text detection. DBNet uses a differentiable binarization module to generate accurate text boundary maps from a ResNet-50 backbone. Compared to traditional approaches such as CRAFT, DBNet provides more efficient detection of continuous text lines and handles curved or tilted text with faster inference speed.
The text detection model is provided by the docTR library (python-doctr), using the pre-trained db_resnet50 architecture. The model outputs relative bounding box coordinates with confidence scores; detections below a confidence threshold of 0.3 are discarded.x
Step 3: Line Segmentation
The detected text regions are then grouped into individual lines using DBSCAN clustering on the vertical center of detected bounding boxes. The clustering radius (eps) is adaptively computed as half the median bounding box height, enabling automatic adjustment to different text sizes. Within each cluster, boxes are sorted left-to-right to establish reading order, this approach effectively handles misaligned and non-uniform handwritten text.
Step 4: Text Recognition
The core component of the proposed system is the TrOCR model, a Transformer-based architecture that performs end-to-end text recognition. It consists of a Vision Transformer (ViT) encoder for extracting visual features and a Transformer-based decoder for generating text sequences.
Unlike traditional CNN-RNN-based methods, TrOCR leverages self-attention mechanisms to capture global contextual dependencies across the entire text line. This enables more robust recognition, particularly for complex handwritten text with irregular spacing and character variations.
To evaluate its effectiveness, TrOCR is compared with baseline models, including CRNN combined with CTC and the SVTR model from PaddleOCR.
Step 5: Deterministic and Language-based Post-processing
To further refine recognition accuracy and mitigate the specific failure modes of the TrOCR architecture, a multi-stage post-processing pipeline is applied.

Deterministic Sanitization: The TrOCR greedy decoder occasionally falls into hallucination loops when encountering high uncertainty, producing infinitely repetitive character sequences. To mitigate this, a deterministic Regex Sanitizer is applied to all raw predictions. This module collapses excessive character repetitions (reducing 4+ consecutive identical characters to a maximum of 3 to preserve valid ellipses) and word-level loops (reducing 3+ identical tokens to 2).

Geographic Entity Correction: A specialized Address Corrector module was developed to refine the recognition of Vietnamese administrative divisions, which often suffer from minor character substitutions. Utilizing a comprehensive gazetteer containing over 10,500 provinces, districts, and wards, the module extracts geographic context using administrative keywords. It employs a 'skip-if-valid' rule to preserve correctly recognized entities and applies Levenshtein distance fuzzy-matching strictly within the detected province scope to correct OCR misspellings.

PhoBERT Rescoring Evaluation: An experimental module using the PhoBERT language model was additionally evaluated to refine linguistic outputs. However, empirical testing on the validation set revealed that PhoBERT Masked Language Modeling (MLM) rescoring acted as a net negative for this specific domain (hurting 6.6 times more samples than it helped). The model frequently 'corrected' valid named entities and alphanumeric codes into common vocabulary words. Consequently, PhoBERT was disabled in the final production pipeline in favor of the deterministic sanitization and domain-specific address correction modules.
EXPERIMENTAL SETUP
Datasets
This study utilizes a large-scale and diverse collection of Vietnamese text datasets, including both handwritten and printed data from multiple real-world and synthetic sources. The datasets are aggregated and standardized into a unified format to support training and evaluation of the proposed OCR system.
Handwritten Datasets
The handwritten data consists of three primary sources:
UIT-HWDB: A large-scale Vietnamese handwriting dataset containing word-, line-, and paragraph-level samples collected from 249 writers. This dataset provides over 110,000 word images and preserves writer identity, enabling writer-independent evaluation.
Cinnamon AI Dataset: A challenging dataset of handwritten Vietnamese addresses, characterized by highly cursive writing styles, complex layouts, and significant background noise.
Viet-Wiki-Handwriting: A synthetic dataset generated from Vietnamese Wikipedia text using handwriting-like fonts. This dataset provides large vocabulary coverage and helps improve language modeling capabilities.
These datasets collectively capture a wide range of handwriting styles, from clean to highly distorted text, ensuring robustness in real-world scenarios.
Printed Text Datasets
The printed text data includes:
VinText: A dataset of natural scene text images with diverse layouts, including curved, rotated, and perspective-distorted text instances.
Vietnamese Receipts (MC-OCR 2021): Real-world receipt images containing noisy backgrounds, low-quality printing, and complex formatting.
Anyuuus OCR Dataset: A large-scale collection of clean printed text lines, suitable for pre-training and learning basic Vietnamese language patterns.
These datasets provide a combination of structured and unstructured printed text under varying imaging conditions.
Data Integration and Standardization
To address the fragmentation of Vietnamese OCR datasets, all data sources are processed through a unified data pipeline. This includes:
Converting images to a consistent RGB format
Normalizing text labels using Unicode NFC
Standardizing annotation formats into a unified CSV schema
Merging datasets into a centralized storage structure
The final dataset supports multiple levels of granularity, including word-level and line-level recognition, for both handwritten and printed text.

Synthetic Printed Data Generation
To address the imbalance and domain bias in the printed text datasets, a synthetic data generation pipeline is introduced to create a large-scale corpus of modern Vietnamese printed text images.
A significant portion of the original printed data originates from historical documents (e.g., Anyuuus dataset), which biases the model toward outdated linguistic styles. This negatively affects generalization to modern Vietnamese text.
To mitigate this issue, more than 30,000 synthetic printed text line images are generated using a dedicated pipeline (see Section 4.2.3).
After integration, the dataset composition is rebalanced as follows:
Historical Vietnamese text: ~34%
Real-world OCR data: ~10%
Synthetic modern printed text: ~56%
This significantly improves linguistic diversity and reduces bias toward historical text.
Dataset Splitting Strategy
To ensure fair evaluation and avoid data leakage, different splitting strategies are applied:
Writer-independent split is used for handwritten datasets (e.g., UIT-HWDB): writers with ID 1–229 are assigned to the training set, writers 230–249 to the validation set, and writers 250–255 to the test set, following the original dataset partition. 
Group-based split: For the Anyuuus OCR dataset, a document-level group split is applied, ensuring that all text lines extracted from the same source document remain within the same subset. The split ratio is 80/10/10 based on unique document identifiers. 
For synthetic datasets, two different split strategies are applied based on their role in training. The Viet-Wiki-Handwriting dataset uses a standard random split of 80/10/10 (train/validation/test). The Synthetic Modern Printed dataset uses a 90/5/5 split to maximize the amount of modern linguistic data available for training, thereby diluting the historical text bias from other printed sources such as the Anyuuus dataset.
All random operations are performed with a fixed seed to ensure reproducibility.
Final Data Representation
The processed datasets are converted into LMDB format for efficient training with the TrOCR model. Separate datasets are maintained for:
Handwritten vs. printed text
Word-level vs. line-level recognition
This design allows flexible training strategies, including staged pretraining and domain adaptation.
Data Preprocessing
Overview
To ensure data quality and improve model generalization, a comprehensive preprocessing pipeline is designed for both printed and handwritten Vietnamese text. The preprocessing stage addresses key challenges such as:
Variability in image resolution and aspect ratio 
Background noise and illumination inconsistency 
Fragmented word-level annotations 
Differences between printed and handwritten text distributions 
The data processing pipeline consists of four main stages: dataset parsing, data splitting, outlier filtering, and LMDB export.
In addition to normalization, a filtering stage is applied to remove noisy samples before training. This includes:
Language filtering based on valid Vietnamese Unicode ranges: A Unicode-based whitelist comprising 10 character ranges is used to validate text labels. Only characters within the ASCII printable range, Latin-1 Supplement, Latin Extended-A/B, Latin Extended Additional (covering all 134 Vietnamese diacritics), General Punctuation, and selected currency/superscript symbols are retained. Labels containing characters from non-Vietnamese scripts — including CJK ideographs, Khmer, Thai, Cyrillic, IPA extensions, and Fullwidth forms — are removed along with their corresponding images. This filtering step is executed before dimension filtering to avoid unnecessary image I/O operations.

Aspect-Ratio-Aware Image Normalization
Instead of enforcing a square input resolution, the preprocessing pipeline adopts a rectangular input representation of size , which better reflects the natural aspect ratio of Vietnamese text lines.
To preserve compatibility with the Vision Transformer (ViT) architecture, the positional embeddings are bicubic-interpolated from the original  grid to a  grid, corresponding to the new patch layout.
Images are stored in LMDB in their original resolution and are not resized during data export. All resizing operations are handled explicitly during model input processing.
Unlike standard TrOCR preprocessing, automatic resizing in the image processor is explicitly disabled (processor.image_processor.do_resize = False) to prevent unintended distortion of the input.
This design ensures that long text sequences are preserved without excessive compression, which is critical for accurate recognition of Vietnamese diacritics, spacing, and character connectivity.
Synthetic Printed Vietnamese Data Generation
To address the imbalance in textual style and improve the generalization capability of the OCR decoder, a synthetic data generation pipeline is introduced to create a large-scale dataset of modern printed Vietnamese text images.
The original training data in Stage 2a is dominated by historical Vietnamese text (approximately 77%), which biases the model toward outdated linguistic patterns. This issue is particularly harmful for real-world OCR applications that require recognition of contemporary language usage. To mitigate this, a synthetic dataset of over 30,000 printed text line images is generated: The underlying text corpus contains approximately 8,000–15,000 unique sentences. Each sentence is rendered multiple times with different fonts and augmentations, following a corpus-cycling strategy that maximizes textual diversity for the decoder while still providing visual variety.

Data Augmentation Strategy
To improve model robustness and simulate real-world document degradation, a lightweight data augmentation pipeline is applied exclusively during training. The pipeline consists of three primary transformations: 
- Color Jitter: Brightness and contrast are randomly adjusted by up to 20%. Saturation and hue are strictly kept at 0.0 to ensure images remain grayscale-equivalent, preventing the model from learning color hallucinations on black-ink text. 
- Adaptive Median Fill Rotation: Images are randomly rotated by up to ±2.0 degrees to simulate natural baseline drift in handwriting and scanned documents. To prevent bright-stripe artifacts when rotating dark background images (such as VinText scene-text), the padding color is dynamically computed as the median value of the border pixels. 
- Gaussian Blur: A 3x3 Gaussian Blur filter with a random sigma (0.1 to 1.0) is applied with a 30% probability to simulate camera defocus and low-resolution scanning artifacts. These transformations are applied dynamically to the fully assembled pseudo-lines in Stage 1, rather than on isolated words, ensuring a realistic representation of line-level degradation.

Model Configuration
This study adopts the Transformer-based Optical Character Recognition architecture, specifically the TrOCR framework, which integrates a Vision Transformer (ViT) encoder and a RoBERTa-based autoregressive decoder. The model is initialized from the pretrained checkpoint microsoft/trocr-base-stage1, which is trained on large-scale document images (IIT-CDIP) without strong language-specific bias, making it suitable for adaptation to Vietnamese text.
Backbone Architecture
The encoder processes input images with a rectangular resolution  instead of the default square format.
The image is divided into a grid of  patches, and positional embeddings are adapted via bicubic interpolation to match this configuration.
This modification significantly improves recognition performance on long text lines compared to square resizing, as it preserves the natural spatial distribution of characters.
The encoder–decoder architecture enables end-to-end training without requiring explicit character segmentation, which is particularly beneficial for handling complex Vietnamese scripts with diacritics.
Tokenizer and Vocabulary Extension
To better support Vietnamese text, the tokenizer is extended with a set of custom tokens covering frequently occurring Vietnamese characters and diacritic combinations. After adding new tokens, the decoder embedding matrix is resized accordingly.
This modification mitigates the issue of suboptimal byte-level tokenization, which can fragment Vietnamese characters into multiple tokens and degrade recognition performance. By introducing language-specific tokens, the model achieves more stable and semantically meaningful decoding.
Decoding Strategy
During inference, greedy decoding (num_beams = 1) is employed instead of beam search.
Although beam search is commonly used to improve sequence generation quality, preliminary experiments revealed that combining beam search with no-repeat n-gram constraints leads to instability in Vietnamese text generation, particularly due to the interaction with diacritic-rich tokens.
Specifically, the use of no_repeat_ngram_size introduces conflicts with valid Vietnamese character compositions, resulting in hallucination and repetitive outputs.
Therefore, the final system adopts a simplified decoding strategy:
num_beams = 1 (greedy search) 
no_repeat_ngram_size = disabled 
length_penalty = 1.0 
This configuration provides more stable and accurate predictions for Vietnamese OCR tasks.
ViT Positional Embedding Interpolation
A key challenge in adapting the pretrained model to Vietnamese text lines is the mismatch in input resolution. The base IIT-CDIP model expects 384×384 square inputs (a 24×24 patch grid), whereas our line-level text requires a 128×1536 rectangular format (an 8×96 patch grid). To accommodate this without destroying the spatial priors learned during pre-training, we reshape the pre-trained 24×24 positional embedding grid to 8×96 using bicubic interpolation. The CLS token embedding is preserved unchanged. This operation, performed before fine-tuning begins, preserves the spatial relationships of the pre-trained weights while adapting the Vision Transformer to the wide rectangular geometry of text lines.
Multi-stage Training Configuration
The model is trained using a multi-stage pipeline designed to progressively increase sequence complexity. Training begins at the word level and gradually transitions to line and paragraph-level recognition.
Each stage employs different data compositions and optimization strategies, enabling the model to learn robust visual features before adapting to long-range textual dependencies. The detailed training procedure is described in Section 4.4.
Training Setup
To effectively adapt the TrOCR framework for Vietnamese text recognition, we design a multi-stage training strategy that progressively increases sequence complexity and mitigates catastrophic forgetting. The training pipeline consists of four stages, transitioning from word-level recognition to paragraph-level understanding.
Training Environment and Reproducibility
All experiments are implemented using PyTorch and the HuggingFace Transformers library. Training is performed on GPU when available to accelerate large-scale data processing.
To ensure reproducibility, a fixed random seed is applied across Python, NumPy, and PyTorch. Additionally, cuDNN benchmarking is enabled to optimize convolution operations for performance.
Stage 1: Curriculum Learning from Word to Pseudo-line
In the first stage, the model is trained on a mixture of printed and handwritten word-level datasets. A curriculum learning strategy is applied to gradually increase sequence length.
Specifically, handwritten samples are dynamically concatenated into pseudo-lines, while printed samples are always kept as isolated words. This design prevents the introduction of artificial seam artifacts in printed text, which can negatively affect the Vision Transformer encoder.
The curriculum is divided into three phases:
Phase 1A: All samples are treated as isolated words. 
Phase 1B: A portion of handwritten samples is concatenated into short pseudo-lines (3–5 words). 
Phase 1C: Most handwritten samples are concatenated into longer pseudo-lines (5–7 words). 
Pseudo-lines are generated using random spacing and vertical jitter to simulate natural handwriting variations. This allows the model to gradually adapt to longer sequences without requiring real line-level annotations.
Unlike conventional staged training strategies, cross-attention layers are not frozen during this stage and remain fully trainable from the beginning of training.
Initial experiments with freezing cross-attention resulted in severe convergence issues, where the model collapsed into repetitive token predictions (BOS saturation). Allowing cross-attention to be trainable from epoch 0 ensures stable optimization and effective alignment between visual features and textual outputs.
Stage 2a: Printed Line Fine-tuning
In the second stage, the model is fine-tuned exclusively on printed line-level data. This stage focuses on stabilizing the decoder for longer sequences under clean and structured conditions.
During this phase, the model learns consistent character spacing and improves alignment between visual features and textual outputs. The training data consists solely of printed text to ensure a stable learning signal before introducing more complex handwritten variations.
At the end of this stage, the Fisher Information Matrix is computed to capture the importance of model parameters. This information is later used in Stage 2b to prevent catastrophic forgetting.
Stage 2b Mixed Training with Replay and EWC
To adapt the model to handwritten line data while preserving knowledge of printed text, we employ a mixed training strategy combined with Elastic Weight Consolidation (EWC).
Each training batch is constructed with a fixed ratio:
70% handwritten samples
30% printed samples (replay buffer)
This ensures that the model continuously revisits printed data while learning handwritten patterns.

Stage 3: Paragraph-level Adaptation
Stage 3 (Paragraph Adaptation) was architecturally designed but was not executed in the final system due to constraints on paragraph-level training data. The model was finalized and evaluated after Stage 2b. The architecture for Stage 3 is preserved for future work.
Optimization and Training Details
The model is trained using standard cross-entropy loss for sequence prediction. Padding tokens are masked during loss computation to avoid affecting gradients. Different batch sizes and learning schedules are applied across stages to match the complexity of the data. In particular:
- Stage-Specific Gradient Accumulation: To accommodate the high memory requirements of the VisionEncoderDecoder architecture on a 24GB VRAM GPU, gradient accumulation is strategically employed. In Stage 1, shorter sequences allow a base batch size of 32 with 4 accumulation steps (effective batch size of 128). In Stage 2, longer line-level sequences require the base batch size to be reduced to 8 with 8 accumulation steps (effective batch size of 64) to prevent Out-Of-Memory (OOM) errors.
- Mixed-Domain Sampling: Mixed-domain batches are carefully controlled using a custom sampler to maintain a fixed ratio of 70% handwritten and 30% printed samples. For the Elastic Weight Consolidation (EWC) applied in Stage 2b, the penalty coefficient (Lambda) was empirically tuned and set to 500.0 to optimally balance this ratio.
- Teacher Forcing: All training stages use teacher forcing, where ground-truth tokens are fed into the decoder during training to stabilize convergence and accelerate early learning.

Layer-wise Learning Rate Decay (LLRD)
Instead of using a uniform learning rate across all model parameters, this work adopts Layer-wise Learning Rate Decay (LLRD) to control the update magnitude of different components in the architecture.
Specifically:
Lower encoder layers are assigned smaller learning rates to preserve pre-trained visual features. 
Higher encoder layers receive moderately larger learning rates to allow adaptation. 
Decoder layers are trained with the base learning rate. 
Cross-attention layers use an intermediate learning rate to balance visual-text alignment. 
This strategy helps prevent catastrophic forgetting in early layers while allowing effective fine-tuning of high-level representations.
Learning Rate Scheduling
A linear warmup followed by cosine decay schedule is applied during training.
Warmup phase gradually increases the learning rate to stabilize early training. 
Cosine decay smoothly reduces the learning rate, allowing fine convergence in later stages. 
This scheduling strategy improves optimization stability and prevents large gradient updates at the beginning of training.
Mixed Precision Training (AMP)
To accelerate training and reduce GPU memory consumption, Automatic Mixed Precision (AMP) is utilized.
Forward and backward computations are performed in FP16 where possible. 
A dynamic gradient scaler is applied to avoid numerical underflow. 
This enables larger batch sizes and faster training without sacrificing model performance.
Gradient Accumulation
Due to hardware limitations, gradient accumulation is used to simulate larger batch sizes.
Gradients are accumulated over multiple mini-batches before updating model weights. 
This improves training stability and convergence, especially for large Transformer models. 
Gradient Clipping
To prevent exploding gradients, gradient norm clipping is applied before each optimizer step.
A maximum gradient norm threshold is enforced. 
This ensures numerical stability during training. 
Elastic Weight Consolidation (EWC)
To mitigate catastrophic forgetting when transitioning from printed text to handwritten data, Elastic Weight Consolidation (EWC) is applied in Stage 2b.
A Fisher Information Matrix is computed after Stage 2a. 
Important parameters for printed text recognition are penalized if they deviate significantly. 
This allows the model to retain performance on printed text while adapting to handwritten text.
Standard EWC implementations frequently fail under mixed-precision training (FP16) due to gradient overflow during the Fisher Information computation. To ensure stability, several engineering adaptations were developed for the EWC module:

First, infinite gradients (inf) and NaN values encountered during the backward pass are dynamically skipped and clamped to prevent numerical corruption of the Fisher Information Matrix. Second, a dummy PyTorch optimizer is instantiated alongside a gradient scaler specifically to manage the AMP backward context, forcing the scaler to reset safely after the Fisher computation phase. These adaptations successfully allowed EWC to compute importance weights over 1,000 parameter updates without triggering FP16 overflow or memory aliasing on the GPU.

Baseline Models
Baseline 1: Raw TrOCR (No Adaptation)
The first baseline evaluates the performance of a pre-trained TrOCR model without any task-specific adaptation. Specifically, we use the TrOCR base model initialized from the microsoft/trocr-base-stage1 checkpoint, which has been pre-trained on large-scale printed text datasets (e.g., IIT-CDIP) but has not been exposed to Vietnamese data.
In this configuration, the model is applied directly to the Vietnamese OCR task without any fine-tuning or architectural modification. The following components of the proposed pipeline are intentionally disabled:
No vocabulary expansion for Vietnamese characters
No Unicode normalization alignment between tokenizer and dataset
No positional embedding interpolation for long text lines
No curriculum learning (word-to-line progression)
No domain adaptation (printed vs. handwritten separation)
No post-processing (e.g., PhoBERT correction or address normalization)
During inference, input images are resized to match the expected input resolution of the model, and predictions are generated using greedy decoding.
Objective
This baseline aims to measure the zero-shot capability of TrOCR when applied to a Vietnamese OCR task. It provides a lower-bound reference for performance, highlighting the gap between general-purpose pre-training and language-specific adaptation.
Analysis
Although TrOCR is a powerful vision-language model, its pre-training is primarily based on English and Latin-script datasets. Vietnamese text introduces several additional challenges:
Complex diacritics and tonal marks
Unicode normalization inconsistencies (NFC vs. NFD forms)
Longer character sequences in line-level OCR
Significant visual variation in handwritten text
Without explicit adaptation, the tokenizer may fail to correctly represent Vietnamese characters, often decomposing them into suboptimal byte-level tokens. This leads to degraded decoding quality and increased character-level errors.
Furthermore, the mismatch between the model’s original positional embedding (trained on 384×384 images) and the long horizontal layout of text lines (e.g., 128×1536) can significantly impair spatial encoding.
Expected Performance
Due to the lack of adaptation, this baseline is expected to produce:
High Character Error Rate (CER)
High Word Error Rate (WER)
Poor generalization across both printed and handwritten domains
In particular, errors are likely to concentrate on:
Diacritic misrecognition
Character substitutions
Incomplete or truncated predictions
Role in the Study
This baseline serves as a critical reference point to quantify the effectiveness of each component in the proposed pipeline. By comparing against this zero-shot setting, we can clearly demonstrate the contribution of:
Vietnamese-specific tokenizer adaptation
Multi-stage training strategy
Domain-aware learning
Post-processing corrections
Overall, Baseline 1 establishes the fundamental necessity of task-specific adaptation for achieving robust Vietnamese OCR performance.

If handwritten samples are more difficult, the model may bias toward them and degrade performance on printed text

Baseline 4: Single-Stage Training (No Multi-Stage Pipeline)
Motivation
The proposed approach is built upon a carefully structured multi-stage training pipeline, where the model progressively learns from word-level data to line-level and finally paragraph-level text. Each stage serves a specific purpose, such as curriculum learning, domain specialization, and catastrophic forgetting mitigation through EWC.
However, this design introduces additional complexity in both implementation and training management. Therefore, Baseline D is constructed to answer a fundamental question:
Is the multi-stage training strategy truly necessary, or can a single unified training process achieve comparable performance?
This baseline removes all stage boundaries and evaluates whether the model can implicitly learn all required representations when exposed to the full dataset simultaneously.
Methodology
In this baseline, the same TrOCR architecture, tokenizer, and preprocessing pipeline are retained to ensure a fair comparison. The key difference lies entirely in the training strategy.
All available training data is merged into a single dataset, including printed and handwritten samples across different granularities such as word-level, line-level, and paragraph-level (if available). Instead of progressing through multiple stages, the model is trained in a single continuous process from initialization to convergence.
No curriculum learning is applied. The model does not start from simpler word-level samples before moving to more complex sequences. Instead, it is exposed to all sequence lengths and structures simultaneously.
Additionally, domain separation is completely removed. Printed and handwritten data are mixed together without any control over their proportions within each batch. As a result, the model must learn to handle domain variability implicitly.
Specialized components from the proposed method are also excluded. The CurriculumCollateFn is not used, meaning no pseudo-line construction is performed. Furthermore, Elastic Weight Consolidation (EWC) is not applied, and no replay mechanism is introduced.
Training is performed using standard mini-batch sampling with random shuffling and optimized using the same cross-entropy loss as in other baselines.
Expected Behavior
This baseline offers a simplified and unified training pipeline, which makes it easier to implement and faster to execute. However, the absence of structured learning introduces several limitations.
First, without curriculum learning, the model is required to learn short and long sequences at the same time. This can make optimization more difficult, especially in early training stages when the model has not yet learned basic character-level patterns.
Second, the lack of domain separation may lead to imbalanced learning. Since printed text is generally cleaner and easier to recognize, the model may bias toward printed data, resulting in weaker performance on handwritten text.
Third, the absence of EWC or any continual learning mechanism means the model has no explicit protection against forgetting or domain interference. Even in a mixed setting, gradients from different domains can conflict with each other, leading to unstable convergence.
Finally, the model does not benefit from any form of progressive specialization, which may limit its ability to generalize across different text structures.
Role in Evaluation
Baseline D plays an important role as a control experiment that removes all training strategies introduced in the proposed method. It isolates the effect of the multi-stage pipeline by providing a direct comparison against a fully unstructured training approach.
Through this baseline, it becomes possible to quantify how much performance improvement is gained from curriculum learning, domain-specific fine-tuning, and continual learning mechanisms.
Summary
Baseline D represents a simplified training paradigm in which the model is trained on all available data in a single stage without any structural guidance.
By comparing this baseline with the proposed multi-stage approach, we can demonstrate that the effectiveness of the system does not solely depend on the model architecture, but heavily relies on the design of the training strategy, particularly the staged progression and domain-aware optimization.
Experimental Design
This section describes the experimental design used to evaluate the effectiveness of the proposed multi-stage Transformer-based OCR framework for Vietnamese text recognition. The design focuses on ensuring fair comparison, reproducibility, and comprehensive analysis across different model configurations and data domains.
Evaluation Protocol
To ensure a consistent and fair evaluation across all models and baselines, a unified evaluation protocol is applied.
All models are evaluated on the same test sets, which are strictly separated from training and validation data. The dataset splitting strategies described in Section 4.1.5 are strictly enforced to avoid data leakage, particularly:
Writer-independent splits for handwritten datasets 
Group-based splits for document-level data 
Fixed random seeds for synthetic datasets 
During evaluation, no data augmentation is applied. All input images are processed using the same preprocessing pipeline to ensure consistency across experiments.
Predictions are generated using greedy decoding unless otherwise specified. Special tokens are removed before computing evaluation metrics.
Compared Models
To comprehensively evaluate the contribution of each component in the proposed pipeline, four baseline configurations and the final proposed model are compared:
Baseline 1 (Raw TrOCR): Zero-shot inference without any adaptation 
Baseline 2 (Printed-only Fine-tuning): Single-stage training on printed data 
Baseline 3 (Mixed Training): Joint training on printed and handwritten data without structured learning 
Baseline 4 (Single-stage Training): Unified training on all data without multi-stage pipeline 
Proposed Model: Full multi-stage training with curriculum learning, domain adaptation, replay sampling, and EWC 
All models share the same backbone architecture (TrOCR-base) and are initialized from the same pre-trained checkpoint to ensure fairness.
Training Consistency
To isolate the effect of training strategies, all experiments are conducted under controlled conditions:
The same tokenizer and vocabulary configuration are used across all models 
Input image resolution and preprocessing steps remain identical 
Optimization settings (optimizer type, base learning rate, scheduler) are kept consistent whenever applicable 
Training epochs are aligned as closely as possible in terms of total update steps 
For baselines that do not use multi-stage training, the total number of training iterations is matched to the proposed model to ensure comparable computational budgets.
Evaluation Metrics and Reporting
Performance is evaluated using two standard OCR metrics:
Character Error Rate (CER) 
Word Error Rate (WER) 
To capture domain-specific behavior, results are reported separately for:
Printed text dataset 
Handwritten text dataset 
This dual-domain evaluation is critical for identifying trade-offs between domains and detecting catastrophic forgetting.
In addition to final performance, intermediate results across training stages are analyzed for the proposed model to study learning dynamics.
Ablation Study Design
To further understand the contribution of individual components in the proposed pipeline, a series of ablation experiments are conducted.
Each experiment removes or modifies a single component while keeping all others unchanged. The key components evaluated include:
Curriculum learning (word → pseudo-line progression) 
Synthetic data generation for printed text 
Mixed sampling strategy (70% handwritten, 30% printed) 
Elastic Weight Consolidation (EWC) 
Positional embedding re-initialization 
Vietnamese tokenizer extension 
This design allows us to quantify the impact of each component on overall system performance.
Computational Efficiency Evaluation
In addition to recognition accuracy, computational efficiency is also considered.
The following aspects are analyzed:
Inference speed (time per image) 
GPU memory consumption during training 
Training time across different stages 
This evaluation helps assess the practical applicability of the proposed model, especially in resource-constrained environments.
Robustness Evaluation
To evaluate model robustness under real-world conditions, additional experiments are conducted on challenging samples, including:
Low-resolution images 
Noisy backgrounds 
Irregular handwritten text 
Images with skew or rotation 
These tests are not used for primary benchmarking but provide qualitative insights into model behavior.
Summary
The experimental design is structured to provide a comprehensive and fair evaluation of the proposed OCR system. By combining baseline comparisons, ablation studies, dual-domain evaluation, and efficiency analysis, the study ensures that performance improvements are well-justified and not attributed to uncontrolled factors.
This design enables a clear demonstration of the effectiveness of the multi-stage training strategy and its contribution to robust Vietnamese text recognition.

Overview of the Worst-Case Samples
This chapter presents a detailed analysis of the 50 worst-performing samples selected from the full evaluation set of 4,477 samples. These samples are ranked based on the highest Character Error Rate (CER) after post-processing.
The purpose of this analysis is not only to quantify errors but also to understand:
Why the model fails in extreme cases 
Which types of text are most difficult 
How effective each stage of the pipeline is (Raw → Sanitized → Corrected) 
What limitations remain in the OCR system 
General Observations
From the dataset, we observe:
The highest CER reaches 0.8261, indicating severe degradation of recognition quality. 
Most worst cases belong to the handwritten domain, confirming that handwriting remains the most challenging OCR scenario. 
Post-processing steps (sanitization and correction) provide limited improvement in extreme failure cases. 
Many errors are systematic rather than random, suggesting model-level weaknesses rather than noise-only issues.
Error Type Categorization
Based on the Error_Category field, errors are grouped into three main types:

Substitution Errors (Most Dominant)
Definition
Substitution errors occur when the model correctly predicts text structure but replaces characters or words with incorrect ones.
Characteristics
Partial correctness in sentence structure 
High semantic distortion in many cases 
Frequent in long structured texts (especially addresses) 
Strong impact from Vietnamese diacritics and spelling variations

Analysis
Almost all key tokens are misrecognized. 
The model confuses: 
Street names 
Administrative units 
Numbers 
Indicates weak language modeling for Vietnamese context. 
Impact
CER ranges from 0.32 to 0.77 
Major contributor to overall OCR degradation 
Hallucination Loop Errors
Definition
This type of error occurs when the model generates repeated or infinite character sequences, often unrelated to the input.
Characteristics
Excessive repetition of characters (e.g., “ĐĐĐĐĐĐ…”) 
Inflated CER values in raw output 
Severe distortion of meaning 
Partially correctable by post-processing 
Example
Raw Output: quân sang đây.....................................................................
After Sanitization: quân sang đây...
Analysis
The model enters a decoding instability state. 
Likely caused by: 
Beam search instability 
Weak stopping criteria 
Overconfidence in partial predictions 
Effectiveness of Pipeline
Sanitization significantly reduces CER (e.g., from 4.25 → 0.71) 
However, semantic completeness is still lost

Definition
Truncation errors occur when the model only predicts a very small fragment of the full text.
Characteristics
Extremely short outputs 
Missing most of the ground truth content 
Often caused by detection or segmentation failure

The model fails to continue decoding after initial tokens.

Impact

Effect of PhoBERT Correction
An experimental post-processing module using PhoBERT (Masked Language Modeling) was evaluated to refine linguistic outputs. However, empirical testing on the validation set revealed that PhoBERT MLM rescoring acted as a net negative for this specific domain (hurting 445 samples while only helping 67, a 6.6:1 regression ratio). The model frequently 'corrected' valid named entities, specialized acronyms, and alphanumeric codes into common vocabulary words. Consequently, PhoBERT was disabled in the final production pipeline in favor of deterministic sanitization and domain-specific address correction.

Root Cause Analysis
Based on error distribution, we identify four major causes:
Input Image Quality Issues
Blurred or noisy images 
Low contrast handwriting 
Poor segmentation accuracy 
Limitations of TrOCR
Not fully optimized for Vietnamese language 
Weak handling of long structured text (e.g., addresses) 
Susceptible to hallucination in uncertain regions 
Decoding Instability
The TrOCR greedy decoder occasionally falls into hallucination loops, producing infinitely repetitive character sequences when it encounters high uncertainty. While the repetition penalty mitigates this, it cannot establish strong termination constraints for severely degraded input.
Lack of strong termination constraints 
Weak Post-processing Strategy
Sanitization is rule-based and shallow 
Correction model lacks contextual grounding for severe errors 
Conclusion
The analysis of the worst-performing samples reveals that:
The dominant error type is substitution error, especially in handwritten text. 
Hallucination errors can be mitigated effectively, but not fully eliminated. 
Truncation errors represent complete recognition failure, typically due to input or detection issues. 
Post-processing improves robustness but is insufficient for severe OCR breakdowns. 
PhoBERT provides limited benefit in extreme error scenarios. 
The system’s performance is primarily constrained by:
handwriting variability 
model generalization limits 
weak structured-text understanding 
Future improvements should focus on data quality, domain-specific training, and stronger decoding strategies, rather than relying only on post-processing.


CONCLUSION AND FUTURE WORK
Conclusion of the Study
This study investigated the performance of Transformer-based architectures, particularly TrOCR, in comparison with traditional CNN-RNN (CRNN + CTC) models for Vietnamese text recognition. The research focused on both handwritten and printed text at the line level, with evaluation based on Character Error Rate (CER) and Word Error Rate (WER), as well as computational efficiency.
Overall, the experimental results indicate that Transformer-based models significantly improve recognition quality, especially in handling complex Vietnamese text with diacritics and contextual dependencies. Compared to CRNN-based models, TrOCR demonstrates better robustness in ambiguous or degraded text regions, where sequential CNN-RNN approaches often fail to capture long-range dependencies.
However, this improvement comes at the cost of increased computational requirements, particularly in inference time and memory usage. This highlights an important trade-off between accuracy and efficiency when deploying Transformer-based models in real-world systems.
